- What is quantum computing?
Quantum computing uses quantum mechanical principles to process information. Instead of classical bits, it uses qubits, which can exist in superpositions of states. This allows quantum computers to perform certain computations in fundamentally different ways from classical computers.

- Why is it different from classical computing?
Classical computers use bits that are either 0 or 1. Quantum computers use qubits, which can exist in superpositions and can exhibit interference. Because of this, quantum computers follow a different computational model rather than simply being faster versions of classical computers.

- What problems might benefit?
Quantum computing may help with problems involving cryptography, chemistry, materials science, optimization, and possibly some machine learning tasks. However, it is not expected to improve every computational problem.

- Why isn't everyone using it already?
Quantum computers are difficult to build and maintain. Qubits are highly sensitive to noise and errors, and current quantum hardware is still limited in size and reliability. For most tasks, classical computers remain more practical and efficient.
